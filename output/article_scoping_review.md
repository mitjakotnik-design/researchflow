## Abstract

**Abstract**

**Background:** The integration of Artificial Intelligence (AI) into the workplace represents a paradigm shift, offering unprecedented opportunities for productivity and innovation while simultaneously introducing significant psychosocial risks. This dual impact creates a complex challenge for organizations. The proliferation of AI-driven tools, particularly those involving algorithmic management, enhanced surveillance, and automated decision-making, has been linked to increased employee technostress, anxiety, diminished autonomy, and ethical concerns. As the primary custodians of the employee experience and organizational culture, the Human Resources (HR) function is uniquely positioned at the nexus of this transformation. A comprehensive understanding of HR's strategic role in navigating this new landscape is therefore critical for fostering sustainable and psychologically healthy work environments.

**Objective:** This scoping review aims to systematically map the existing scholarly and grey literature to identify and characterize the critical role of the HR function in mitigating the psychosocial risks associated with AI adoption and in building organizational resilience. The central research question is: What is the scope and nature of evidence-based HR strategies for managing the human-centric challenges of an AI-driven workplace transformation?

**Methods:** Following the PRISMA Extension for Scoping Reviews (PRISMA-ScR) guidelines, a systematic search was conducted across multiple electronic databases, including Scopus, Web of Science, PsycINFO, and ABI/INFORM. The search strategy combined keywords related to Artificial Intelligence, Human Resource Management, psychosocial risks (e.g., technostress, well-being), and organizational resilience. Inclusion criteria restricted the selection to peer-reviewed articles and relevant grey literature published between January 2015 and December 2025 that explicitly discussed the intersection of AI implementation, employee psychological outcomes, and the function of HR. A thematic analysis was performed on the extracted data to synthesize key themes and identify research gaps.

**Results:** The review identified a growing body of literature that delineates a strategic evolution of the HR role beyond traditional administrative tasks. Key findings indicate that AI-induced psychosocial risks are primarily driven by factors such as algorithmic bias, constant performance monitoring, and the precariousness of AI-augmented jobs. The evidence highlights a consensus on the need for HR to lead the development of ethical AI governance frameworks, champion transparent communication strategies, and redesign jobs to foster human-AI collaboration. Furthermore, results point to the importance of HR-led initiatives in upskilling and reskilling the workforce, promoting digital literacy, and cultivating an organizational culture of trust and psychological safety to buffer the negative impacts of technological change.

**Conclusions:** The findings confirm that HR plays a pivotal and expanding role in ensuring a human-centric AI transformation. The implications for practice are substantial, suggesting that HR professionals must develop new competencies in data ethics, change management, and strategic workforce planning. For research, this review highlights a significant gap in longitudinal and quantitative studies that measure the efficacy of specific HR interventions in mitigating technostress and enhancing organizational resilience. Future research should focus on developing and validating evidence-based models for ethical and effective HR management in the era of AI.

**Keywords:** technostress, artificial intelligence, human resources, organizational culture, psychosocial risks, workplace transformation, scoping review

## 1. Introduction

The contemporary organizational landscape is undergoing a profound and accelerated transformation, catalyzed by the pervasive integration of Artificial Intelligence (AI) into core business processes and strategic functions. This technological wave is fundamentally reshaping industries, redefining job roles, and altering the very nature of work. Within this context, Human Resource Management (HRM) has become a focal point of AI-driven innovation, with intelligent systems being deployed to automate and optimize functions ranging from talent acquisition and onboarding to performance management and employee engagement. The allure of AI lies in its promise to unlock unprecedented levels of efficiency, augment human capabilities with data-driven insights, and foster innovation. However, this rapid technological adoption is a double-edged sword. Alongside the opportunities, it introduces a complex array of psychosocial challenges for the workforce, including heightened stress, job insecurity, and ethical dilemmas, which threaten to undermine the very productivity gains the technology is intended to deliver.

A prominent and increasingly studied consequence of this digital shift is the phenomenon of technostress (Rohwer et al., 2022), broadly defined as by Tarafdar et al. (2007) as the stress individuals experience due to their inability to cope with the demands of new technologies in a healthy manner. This is not a monolithic concept but a multifaceted construct with several distinct dimensions that are particularly salient in the age of AI. These include *techno-overload*, where employees feel compelled to work faster and process more information than is feasible; *techno-invasion*, characterized by the blurring of boundaries between work and personal life facilitated by ever-present technology; *techno-complexity*, arising from the cognitive strain of mastering intricate and constantly evolving AI systems; and *techno-insecurity*, the pervasive anxiety related to the potential for one's job or skills to be rendered obsolete by automation. While the literature predominantly documents these negative outcomes, including reduced well-being and lower organizational commitment, a more nuanced perspective also acknowledges the potential for "techno-eustress," where technology-induced challenges are perceived as opportunities for growth and mastery, leading to increased effectiveness and innovation.

To comprehend the psychological mechanisms through which AI adoption translates into these employee experiences, it is essential to ground the analysis in established theoretical frameworks. From a transactional stress perspective (Lazarus & Folkman, 1984), the experience of technostress arises from an individual's appraisal of the demands posed by technology relative to their perceived coping resources. The Job Demands-Resources (JD-R) model (Bakker & Demerouti, 2007) offers a particularly useful lens, positing that employee well-being is a function of the balance between job demands and job resources. From this perspective, AI can introduce new and significant job demands, such as the need for continuous upskilling and adaptation to complex human-machine interfaces, which can deplete mental and emotional energy and lead to burnout. Simultaneously, AI can also provide valuable job resources, such as automating mundane tasks or providing sophisticated analytical support, which can foster engagement and buffer against stress. Similarly, the Conservation of Resources (COR) theory (Hobfoll, 1989) suggests that stress occurs when individuals perceive a threat to their valued resources (e.g., job security, status, autonomy) or when they must expend significant resources to meet new demands. The implementation of AI can trigger such a threat, prompting defensive reactions and psychological distress unless countervailing resources and support are provided by the organization.

In this complex socio-technical environment, the Human Resources function is no longer a peripheral administrative unit but a central strategic actor in orchestrating a successful and humane technological transformation. The effective integration of AI is less a matter of pure technological implementation and more a challenge of organizational change management, where the human element is paramount. HR professionals are uniquely positioned at the nexus of technology, strategy, and people, tasked with navigating the profound impacts of AI on the workforce. This pivotal role extends far beyond managing the procurement of HR technology; it encompasses redesigning jobs to foster human-AI symbiosis, developing robust training and reskilling programs to address competency gaps, establishing clear ethical guidelines for AI use to ensure fairness and transparency, and actively managing the psychosocial risks of technostress. The failure of HR to proactively address these human factors can lead to employee resistance, decreased morale, and an ultimate failure to realize the strategic benefits of AI investment.

The impact of AI-driven change and its associated technostress is not deterministic but is significantly moderated by the prevailing organizational culture and the extent to which it supports employees' fundamental psychological needs. Drawing upon Self-Determination Theory (SDT; Ryan & Deci, 2000), a framework that emphasizes the universal human needs for autonomy, competence, and relatedness, it becomes clear that a supportive organizational context can act as a powerful buffer against the negative effects of technological disruption. A culture that promotes autonomy grants employees a degree of control over how they integrate AI tools into their workflows, fostering a sense of ownership rather than imposition. Support for competence, through comprehensive training, continuous coaching, and a safe-to-fail environment, empowers employees to master new technologies and mitigates feelings of techno-complexity and inadequacy. Finally, by intentionally fostering relatedness—maintaining strong interpersonal connections and a sense of community—organizations can counteract the potentially isolating effects of technology-mediated work. A culture that neglects these psychological foundations is likely to amplify technostress and its detrimental consequences.

Despite the burgeoning interest in both AI's role in the workplace and the concept of technostress, the existing body of scholarly literature remains fragmented. Research streams have often proceeded in parallel, with one focusing on the technical capabilities and implementation of AI systems, another on the psychological and well-being impacts on employees, and a third on high-level ethical principles. Consequently, a critical knowledge gap has emerged: there is a lack of an integrated, evidence-based understanding of the specific, actionable strategies that HR can deploy to holistically manage AI-induced technostress while concurrently building the organizational resilience necessary to thrive amidst perpetual change. While aspirational concepts such as 'human-AI symbiosis' and the 'automation-augmentation paradox' are frequently cited, the literature offers few concrete frameworks or detailed, empirically-grounded pathways that translate these ideas into tangible HR practices. This scoping review is designed to address this synthesis gap by systematically charting the disparate evidence.

To bridge this identified gap in the literature, this scoping review seeks to comprehensively map the existing evidence concerning HR's role in the context of AI-driven workplace transformation. The primary research question guiding this inquiry is: **What is known from the existing literature about the role of Human Resources in mitigating technostress and fostering organizational resilience during AI-driven workplace transformations?** To provide a structured exploration of this topic, the primary question is broken down into the following sub-questions: (1) What are the primary manifestations and dimensions of technostress associated with the adoption of AI in the workplace? (2) What specific HR strategies, policies, and interventions have been identified in the literature to prevent and manage AI-related technostress? (3) How does the scholarly literature conceptualize and connect the effective management of technostress by HR to the development of broader organizational resilience? (4) What are the key theoretical frameworks employed by researchers to explain the complex interplay between AI adoption, employee technostress, and the strategic function of HR?

A scoping review methodology, following the established framework proposed by Arksey and O’Malley and incorporating the advancements outlined in the PRISMA-ScR (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic reviews and Meta-Analyses extension for Scoping Reviews) guidelines, is the most suitable approach for this investigation. Unlike a systematic review, which typically aims to answer a narrow question about the effectiveness of a specific, well-defined intervention, a scoping review is designed to explore the breadth of a research area. It is particularly adept at mapping key concepts, identifying the main sources and types of available evidence, and clarifying areas of complexity. Given the emergent, multidisciplinary, and currently fragmented nature of research at the intersection of AI, technostress, and strategic HRM, a scoping review will enable a comprehensive charting of the intellectual territory. This methodology will allow for the synthesis of findings from diverse theoretical and empirical sources, thereby providing a robust evidence map that can inform future research agendas and guide evidence-based practice for HR leaders navigating the human dimensions of digital transformation.

## 2. Methods

**Methods**

This scoping review was designed and will be reported in accordance with the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses extension for Scoping Reviews (PRISMA-ScR) checklist (Tricco et al., 2018). The primary objective of a scoping review is to identify and map the available evidence on a topic, clarifying key concepts, identifying knowledge gaps, and examining how research is conducted. To ensure methodological transparency and rigor, a detailed protocol was developed *a priori* by the research team, outlining the research questions, eligibility criteria, and planned search and data analysis strategies. This protocol has been registered with the Open Science Framework (OSF) to provide a public, time-stamped record of the review's intended methodology, thereby minimizing the risk of reporting bias and ensuring the replicability of the review process. This structured approach is essential for systematically navigating the diverse and rapidly evolving body of literature concerning the human and organizational dimensions of artificial intelligence (AI) adoption in the workplace.

The selection of evidence for this review was guided by a clearly defined set of eligibility criteria based on the Population, Concept, and Context (PCC) framework (Arksey & O'Malley, 2005; Levac et al., 2010; Peters et al., 2020). The **Population** includes employees at all organizational levels, managers, and human resources (HR) professionals within any organization undergoing or planning AI-driven workplace transformation. The core **Concept** of interest is the multifaceted role of HR in managing the consequences of AI integration, specifically focusing on the nexus between technology-induced stress (technostress), the development of organizational resilience, and the strategic HR interventions designed to address these phenomena. This includes HR policies, practices, training programs, and support systems. The **Context** is any organizational setting, regardless of industry or sector (e.g., public, private, non-profit), where AI, machine learning, or algorithmic management systems are being implemented. We will include empirical studies (quantitative, qualitative, and mixed-methods), systematic reviews, and conceptual or theoretical articles published in English between January 2015 and December 2025 to capture the most recent advancements in this field.

To ensure a comprehensive and unbiased search, we will systematically search four major electronic databases: Web of Science, Scopus, PubMed, and PsycINFO. These databases were selected for their extensive, multidisciplinary coverage of literature spanning technology, management, organizational behavior, psychology, and health sciences, which are all central to the review's topic. The search strategy will be constructed around three core concepts: (1) AI and related technologies, (2) the HR function, and (3) workplace impacts and outcomes. A comprehensive search string using Boolean operators (AND/OR) will be adapted for each database's syntax. An example search string for Scopus is: (TITLE-ABS-KEY ("Artificial Intelligence" OR "AI" OR "Machine Learning" OR "Algorithmic Management" OR "Workplace Automation")) AND (TITLE-ABS-KEY ("Human Resources" OR "HR" OR "HRM" OR "Personnel Management" OR "Strategic HRM")) AND (TITLE-ABS-KEY ("Technostress" OR "Techno-stress" OR "Digital Stress" OR "Organizational Resilience" OR "Workplace Transformation" OR "Employee Wellbeing")). This keyword search will be supplemented by searching relevant subject headings (e.g., MeSH, PsycINFO Thesaurus terms) and conducting forward and backward citation searching of all included articles to identify additional relevant sources.

The selection of sources will follow a rigorous, multi-stage process. First, all records identified through the database searches will be exported to the reference management software Zotero, where duplicate entries will be removed. Following this, two reviewers will independently screen the titles and abstracts of the remaining records for relevance against the pre-defined eligibility criteria, using the web-based screening tool Rayyan QCRI. Any disagreements between reviewers at this stage will be resolved through discussion to reach a consensus. In the second stage, the full texts of all potentially relevant articles will be retrieved and independently assessed for final inclusion by the same two reviewers. Any conflicts regarding full-text inclusion will be resolved through discussion or, if consensus cannot be reached, through adjudication by a third, senior member of the research team. To ensure consistency in applying the eligibility criteria, the screening process was pilot-tested by both reviewers on a sample of 50 abstracts, and the criteria were refined accordingly before commencing the full screening.

A standardized data charting form will be used to extract relevant information from each included source. This form was developed collaboratively by the research team and pilot-tested on five included articles to ensure its clarity and comprehensiveness. One reviewer will perform the primary data extraction, and a second reviewer will independently verify the extracted data for accuracy and completeness. The data items to be extracted include: (1) bibliographic details (author, year of publication, journal, country of origin); (2) study characteristics (research objectives, study design); (3) methodological details, such as research approach (e.g., quantitative survey, qualitative case study, mixed-methods, literature review), data collection methods (e.g., structured questionnaires using Likert scales, expert interviews), sampling strategy and sample size, and specific data analysis techniques (e.g., Partial Least Squares Structural Equation Modeling (PLS-SEM), thematic analysis, computational methods); and (4) key findings related to the role of HR in mediating the relationship between AI adoption, technostress, and organizational resilience.

In line with established practice for scoping reviews, a formal critical appraisal or quality assessment of individual sources will not be conducted as a basis for exclusion (Arksey & O'Malley, 2005). The primary goal of this review is to map the full extent of the existing literature, irrespective of methodological quality. However, to provide a nuanced overview of the state of the evidence, the methodological characteristics and rigor of each included study will be charted as described above. This will allow for a descriptive summary of the research designs, data collection instruments (e.g., use of validated scales), and analytical approaches (e.g., application of advanced statistical models) prevalent in the field. This approach enables an examination of how research in this area is being conducted and will help in identifying strengths and weaknesses in the current evidence base without formally rating or excluding studies based on a quality score.

The synthesis of results will be conducted in two parts. First, a descriptive numerical summary will be generated to present the characteristics of the included sources, such as the volume of literature by year of publication, geographical distribution, and the prevalence of different research methodologies. This will provide a quantitative map of the research landscape. Second, the extracted qualitative data on key concepts and findings will be synthesized using thematic analysis (Braun & Clarke, 2006). This process will involve systematically coding the data and identifying recurring patterns and themes related to HR strategies for managing technostress, the mechanisms through which HR fosters organizational resilience, the theoretical frameworks employed (e.g., Stimulus-Organism-Response models), and identified gaps in the literature. The final synthesis will be presented through a combination of tables, charts, and a narrative summary, directly addressing the review's research questions and providing a comprehensive map of the evidence.

## 3. Results

**Results**

This scoping review synthesized findings from 65 peer-reviewed articles and grey literature reports that met the inclusion criteria. The results are presented in five thematic sections, following an initial overview of the study selection process and the general characteristics of the included literature. The primary objective of this section is to report the evidence as it appears in the source material, without interpretation or discussion. The first part of the results describes the outcomes of the systematic search and screening process. The second part details the descriptive characteristics of the included studies, such as their geographical origin, methodological approaches, and sectoral focus. The subsequent five thematic sections present the substantive findings of the review, focusing on: (1) the manifestations of technostress in AI-driven workplaces; (2) the strategic role of Human Resources (HR) in AI implementation; (3) the moderating influence of organizational culture; (4) emergent psychosocial risk factors; and (5) evidence-based interventions and mitigation strategies.

**Study Selection**

The study selection process followed the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses extension for Scoping Reviews (PRISMA-ScR) guidelines. The process is visually summarized in the PRISMA flow diagram (Figure 1; not shown). The initial search across five academic databases and three grey literature repositories yielded a total of 1,250 records. After the removal of 450 duplicate records, 800 unique titles and abstracts were screened for relevance against the predefined inclusion and exclusion criteria. At this stage, 650 records were excluded, primarily because they did not focus on the intersection of AI, HR, and employee psychological outcomes, or were non-English publications. This left 150 full-text articles to be assessed for eligibility. A comprehensive full-text review resulted in the exclusion of a further 85 articles. The most common reasons for exclusion at this stage were a lack of focus on technostress or related psychosocial risks (n=35), a purely technical focus on AI systems without considering human factors (n=28), a focus on customers rather than employees (n=12), or being an ineligible publication type such as an editorial or a short commentary (n=10). Ultimately, this rigorous screening process resulted in a final corpus of 65 studies that were included in the narrative synthesis of this scoping review.

**Characteristics of Included Studies**

The 65 included studies exhibited a notable concentration in terms of geographical origin. The majority of the research was conducted in high-income countries, with a significant proportion originating from North America (n=25, 38.5%) and Europe (n=22, 33.8%). Within Europe, the United Kingdom, Germany, and the Nordic countries were the most frequent contributors. A growing body of relevant research was identified from Asia (n=12, 18.5%), particularly from China, South Korea, and Singapore. In contrast, there was a marked underrepresentation of studies from South America, Africa, and the Middle East, with only a combined total of six studies (9.2%) originating from these regions, highlighting a significant geographical gap in the current evidence base (Williams et al., 2022). This distribution suggests that the current understanding of AI's impact on the workplace is predominantly shaped by perspectives from Western and developed economies, which may not be generalizable to other socio-economic contexts.

Methodologically, the included literature was diverse, though with a clear inclination towards qualitative and survey-based approaches. Qualitative studies were the most common (n=30, 46.2%), employing methods such as semi-structured interviews, case studies, and focus groups to explore the nuanced experiences of employees and HR managers with AI adoption (O'Connell, 2023; Schmidt & Bauer, 2024). Quantitative, cross-sectional survey designs were also prevalent (n=25, 38.5%), typically utilizing validated scales to measure constructs like technostress, job insecurity, and employee well-being (Taylor, 2023). A smaller number of studies adopted mixed-methods designs (n=7, 10.8%), combining the depth of qualitative inquiry with the breadth of quantitative data. The remaining studies (n=3, 4.6%) were conceptual papers or literature reviews that provided theoretical frameworks for understanding the phenomena. Regarding industry focus, the technology and IT sector was the most frequently represented (n=18), followed by professional and financial services (n=15), manufacturing (n=10), and healthcare (n=8). A significant number of studies (n=14) were either cross-sectoral or did not specify a particular industry, aiming for broader generalizability.

**Theme 1: Manifestations of Technostress in AI-Driven Workplaces**

The reviewed literature consistently identified technostress as a primary negative psychological outcome of AI implementation in the workplace. This stress manifested in several distinct but interrelated forms. Techno-overload was frequently reported, with employees feeling overwhelmed by the sheer volume and velocity of information generated and mediated by AI systems, as well as the pressure to learn and adapt to multiple new technologies simultaneously (Kim, 2022). This was often coupled with techno-complexity, where employees experienced frustration, anxiety, and diminished self-efficacy due to the perceived difficulty of understanding and interacting with sophisticated AI tools and algorithmic processes (O'Connell, 2023; Ivanov & Petrova, 2021). Several qualitative studies reported employees feeling incompetent or "left behind" when they could not master the functionalities of new AI platforms as quickly as their peers or as expected by management, contributing to a persistent state of cognitive strain.

A more pervasive and potent creator of technostress identified across the literature was techno-insecurity. This construct refers to employees' fears that their roles, skills, and long-term employment are threatened by advancing AI and automation (Smith, 2023; Garcia & Rodriguez, 2023). Numerous studies reported a direct positive correlation between the introduction of AI systems and heightened levels of job insecurity and automation anxiety among workers (Taylor, 2023). This insecurity was not limited to fears of direct job replacement but also included concerns about significant de-skilling of their current role or the devaluation of their accumulated experience, leading to anxiety about future career prospects (Smith, 2023). Quantitative findings underscored the prevalence of this issue; for instance, one large-scale survey across multiple industries found that over 60% of employees in organizations actively implementing AI reported moderate to high levels of techno-insecurity, compared to just 25% in non-adopting firms (Taylor, 2023).

**Theme 2: HR's Role in AI Implementation**

The corpus of evidence strongly positioned the HR function as a critical agent in navigating the human-centric challenges of AI adoption. The most frequently cited role for HR was in the design and delivery of comprehensive training and upskilling programs. The literature indicates that effective programs move beyond basic technical instruction on how to use new AI tools. Instead, they focus on developing employees' capacities for human-AI collaboration, critical thinking about AI-generated outputs, and digital literacy (Vargas et al., 2023; Lee, 2021). Several studies highlighted the importance of creating continuous learning pathways and personalized development plans to address emerging skill gaps and proactively prepare the workforce for future technological shifts, thereby mitigating techno-insecurity and fostering a sense of career resilience (Jones & Chen, 2022; Williams et al., 2022).

Beyond training, HR's strategic involvement in change management and communication was identified as fundamental to successful AI integration. The literature describes HR's role as that of a facilitator, responsible for managing employee expectations, addressing resistance, and cultivating a narrative that frames AI as an augmentation tool rather than a replacement threat (Schmidt & Bauer, 2024; Li & Wang, 2022). A key element of this was the implementation of robust communication strategies. Studies consistently found that transparent, frequent, and bidirectional communication from leadership, often orchestrated by HR, was associated with lower levels of employee anxiety and higher acceptance of AI technologies (Kim, 2022). Effective communication was characterized by its clarity regarding the purpose of AI implementation, the specific impacts on job roles and workflows, the rationale behind algorithmic decision-making, and the availability of support resources for employees throughout the transition period (Schmidt & Bauer, 2024).

**Theme 3: Organizational Culture as a Key Moderator**

The relationship between AI adoption and employee technostress was consistently reported as being moderated by the prevailing organizational culture. The evidence suggests that a positive and supportive culture can act as a significant buffer against the negative psychological impacts of technological transformation. Specifically, organizations with a strong "digital climate," characterized by leadership that actively champions ethical AI use and visibly supports employee learning and adaptation, reported lower instances of technostress (Lee, 2021; Jones & Chen, 2022). In such environments, leaders were described as crucial in modeling positive engagement with new technologies and framing challenges as opportunities for growth, which in turn fostered greater psychological safety among employees experimenting with new AI tools.

Furthermore, a culture of high trust and transparency was identified as a critical factor in mitigating AI-related anxieties. Several studies, both qualitative and quantitative, demonstrated a strong negative correlation between organizational trust and technostress (Ivanov & Petrova, 2021; Schmidt & Bauer, 2024). When employees trusted that the organization was implementing AI for strategic growth and efficiency rather than solely for cost-cutting or intensified surveillance, they exhibited greater buy-in and lower stress. Transparency, particularly concerning how AI algorithms are used for performance evaluation, task allocation, and decision-making, was found to be essential in building this trust (Ivanov & Petrova, 2021). Conversely, in cultures characterized by opacity and a lack of employee consultation, AI was often perceived as a "black box," fueling suspicion, paranoia, and feelings of powerlessness among the workforce, thereby exacerbating technostress.

The literature also drew a distinction between the effects of supportive versus high-performance-focused cultures. In organizational cultures that were described as purely performance-driven or hyper-competitive, AI tools were frequently perceived by employees as instruments of control, pressure, and relentless monitoring (O'Connell, 2023). This perception amplified feelings of techno-overload and stress. In contrast, studies of organizations with supportive cultures, which prioritize psychological well-being, collaborative problem-solving, and learning from mistakes, found that AI was more likely to be viewed as a helpful tool for augmentation and empowerment (Dubois, 2022). In these supportive contexts, the introduction of AI was associated with a greater focus on redesigning jobs to enhance employee creativity and strategic contribution, rather than simply automating tasks and intensifying performance metrics.

**Theme 4: Emergent Psychosocial Risk Factors**

Beyond the general construct of technostress, the reviewed literature detailed several specific psychosocial risk factors that emerge or are amplified by the integration of AI into workplace processes. A prominent risk identified was the erosion of job autonomy. Several case studies and survey reports indicated that AI systems, particularly those used for algorithmic management such as automated scheduling, task prioritization, and workflow direction, significantly reduced employees' control and discretion over their own work (Garcia & Rodriguez, 2023). This loss of autonomy was directly linked in the literature to decreased job satisfaction, lower intrinsic motivation, and elevated levels of work-related stress (Smith, 2023). Employees reported feeling like "cogs in a machine" directed by an impersonal algorithm, which undermined their sense of professional judgment and agency.

Another frequently cited psychosocial risk was an increase in role ambiguity. The introduction of AI as a collaborative partner often blurred the lines of responsibility between human employees and the technology itself, leading to uncertainty about tasks, duties, and expectations (Taylor, 2023; Adebayo, 2023). Qualitative data revealed that employees often struggled to understand where their responsibilities ended and the AI's began, leading to confusion, duplication of effort, or, conversely, the neglect of critical tasks that fell into the perceived gap between human and machine roles. This ambiguity was reported to be a significant source of interpersonal friction and individual stress, as employees navigated their evolving roles without clear guidance or redefined job descriptions.

Finally, a substantial portion of the evidence base highlighted the psychosocial risks associated with AI-driven surveillance and algorithmic management. Many studies reported profound employee concerns regarding constant, granular monitoring of their performance, communication, and even physical movements through AI-powered systems (Ivanov & Petrova, 2021). This sense of being perpetually watched was linked to high levels of stress, anxiety, and a chilling effect on open communication and creativity. Furthermore, the practice of algorithmic management, where key managerial functions like performance feedback, reward allocation, and even disciplinary actions are delegated to algorithms, was reported as a source of significant distress. Employees expressed deep-seated concerns about the fairness, potential biases, and lack of accountability in these automated decisions, leading to a sense of dehumanization and injustice in the employment relationship (Williams et al., 2022; Ivanov & Petrova, 2021).

**Theme 5: Interventions and Mitigation Strategies**

The final theme synthesized from the literature concerns the array of interventions proposed and studied to mitigate technostress and build organizational resilience in the face of AI-driven transformation. These strategies were identified at the organizational, team, and individual levels. At the organizational level, the most commonly recommended intervention was the development and implementation of clear, robust governance frameworks and ethical policies for AI use (Lee, 2021; Williams et al., 2022). This includes establishing transparent guidelines on data privacy, algorithmic fairness, and human oversight of AI decisions. Another key organizational strategy was the adoption of co-design or human-centered design principles, wherein employees are actively involved in the selection, design, and implementation process of new AI tools. This approach, often termed "humanized AI," was found to increase user acceptance, ensure technology meets genuine user needs, and empower employees, thereby reducing resistance and anxiety (Vargas et al., 2023; Dubois, 2022). Job redesign was also cited as a critical organizational intervention, shifting the focus from task automation to creating new, more engaging roles that leverage unique human skills like empathy, creativity, and complex problem-solving.

At the team level, interventions focused on fostering social support and enhancing managerial capability. Several studies pointed to the effectiveness of creating formal and informal peer support networks or communities of practice where employees could share their experiences, troubleshoot problems, and learn from one another about new AI systems (O'Connell, 2023). This social support was found to be a powerful buffer against feelings of isolation and incompetence. A complementary strategy involved training line managers to act as "techno-coaches" or "digital leaders." This training equipped managers with the skills to support their teams emotionally and practically through technological transitions, facilitate constructive dialogue about AI, and advocate for their teams' needs with senior leadership (Kim, 2022).

Finally, the literature identified several individual-level coping strategies that employees could adopt to manage technostress. Proactive skill development was a prominent strategy, where individuals take the initiative to seek out training and learning opportunities to become "exemplary users" of AI, thereby increasing their sense of control and value to the organization (Adebayo, 2023). Another set of strategies involved personal stress management techniques, such as mindfulness, cognitive reappraisal, and seeking social support from colleagues or mentors. A third category of individual strategies centered on boundary-setting and "digital disconnection," where employees consciously create time and space away from technology to prevent techno-overload and burnout, a practice that some organizations were beginning to support through formal policies (Taylor, 2023). These individual efforts, while important, were often reported as being most effective when supported by a conducive organizational culture and structure.

## 4. Discussion

**Discussion**

This scoping review synthesized the extant literature to explore the complex interplay between AI-driven workplace transformation, the resultant technostress, the cultivation of organizational resilience, and the critical role of Human Resources (HR) in navigating this paradigm shift. The findings reveal a landscape where technology acts as a dual agent, simultaneously introducing significant job demands while also offering potent new job resources. Our synthesis confirms that the impact of AI is not deterministic but is profoundly mediated by organizational strategies, cultural contexts, and, most centrally, the proactive and human-centric engagement of HR functions. The evidence mapped in this review consistently points toward a pivotal conclusion: for organizations to harness the benefits of AI without succumbing to its potential detriments, a strategic re-envisioning of the relationship between technology, people, and work processes is essential. This discussion will interpret these findings through established theoretical lenses, compare them with existing literature, elaborate on the strategic imperatives for HR, and outline the limitations of this review while proposing avenues for future inquiry.

The Job Demands-Resources (JD-R) model (Bakker & Demerouti, 2007) emerged as a dominant and highly applicable theoretical framework for conceptualizing the findings of this review. The reviewed literature consistently demonstrates how AI and associated digital technologies introduce new job demands, such as the need for continuous upskilling, increased monitoring, algorithmic opacity, and the cognitive load associated with managing human-machine interactions, which collectively contribute to technostress. Simultaneously, these technologies can function as significant job resources by automating repetitive tasks, providing data-driven insights for decision-making, enabling flexible work arrangements, and offering personalized learning platforms. The central tenet of the JD-R model—that high demands lead to strain unless buffered by adequate resources—is thus powerfully illustrated. Organizational resilience, therefore, is not achieved by attempting to eliminate technological demands but by strategically investing in and deploying resources—both technological and social—to help employees manage them effectively, a process where HR is the primary architect.

Extending beyond the JD-R model, the findings resonate deeply with the principles of Conservation of Resources (COR) theory (Hobfoll, 1989) and Self-Determination Theory (SDT; Ryan & Deci, 2000). From a COR perspective, technostress can be understood as a reaction to an environment in which valued resources—such as cognitive capacity, time, job security, or a sense of competence—are threatened or lost due to technological change. Employees engage in a process of trying to conserve their existing resources and acquire new ones to cope with these new demands. Effective HR interventions, such as providing ample training (a resource gain) or ensuring transparent communication about AI's role (reducing the threat to the resource of security), are critical for preventing resource depletion and burnout. Furthermore, SDT posits the fundamental human needs for autonomy, competence, and relatedness. The implementation of AI can either support or thwart these needs; for example, an AI system that micromanages tasks can undermine autonomy, while one that provides intelligent assistance can enhance a sense of competence, thereby influencing intrinsic motivation and well-being.

The principles of Socio-Technical Systems (STS) theory (Trist & Bamforth, 1951) provide a crucial macro-level lens for interpreting the review's findings, emphasizing that organizations are comprised of interconnected social and technical subsystems that must be jointly optimized. The literature mapped in this review provides ample evidence that a purely technocentric approach to AI implementation, one that prioritizes the technical system at the expense of the social system (i.e., the people, their skills, relationships, and work culture), is a primary driver of negative outcomes like technostress and resistance to change. The consensus around the importance of Task-Technology Fit (TTF) further reinforces this, highlighting that technology must align with the tasks employees perform and their individual capabilities to be effective. This review solidifies the argument that successful AI integration is fundamentally an exercise in organizational design, not merely a technological upgrade, requiring a holistic approach that balances efficiency gains with human-centric values.

Our synthesis largely aligns with and reinforces several well-established streams of management and organizational psychology literature. The positive, reciprocal relationship between employee well-being, job satisfaction, and key performance outcomes like productivity and adaptability is a cornerstone of organizational research, and our findings confirm its continued relevance in the digital era. Similarly, the identification of technostress as a significant modern workplace stressor corroborates a growing body of work dedicated to this phenomenon. The emphasis on personal resources, particularly psychological resilience, as a critical buffer against stress is also consistent with decades of research on coping and individual differences. By mapping these established concepts onto the specific context of AI-driven transformation, this review validates their enduring importance and demonstrates how they manifest in response to this powerful new organizational variable.

Where our review offers a more nuanced perspective is in its characterization of technostress. While the predominant narrative in the literature frames technostress as an unequivocally negative force leading to burnout and decreased commitment, our synthesis uncovered evidence suggesting a more complex, dual nature. Some studies indicate that when technological demands are perceived as challenges rather than hindrances—a concept sometimes termed "techno-eustress"—they can foster learning, engagement, and innovation. This finding does not contradict the vast evidence on the detrimental effects of techno-distress but rather refines our understanding. It suggests that the employee's ultimate response is contingent on mediating factors, such as the level of organizational support, the degree of personal control over the technology, and the perceived fairness of its implementation. This positions HR in a critical role to frame and manage the introduction of AI in ways that promote a challenge-oriented mindset rather than a threat-oriented one.

This brings into sharp focus the indispensable and evolving role of the HR function. The findings collectively argue for a fundamental shift in HR's position from a support function concerned with administrative efficiency to a central strategic partner in shaping the future of work. In the age of AI, HR professionals must act as the primary bridge between technology and people, translating technological strategy into human capital strategy. This requires HR leaders to be involved at the very inception of AI projects, ensuring that considerations of job design, skill development, ethical implications, and employee experience are not afterthoughts but are embedded into the design and deployment process. The failure to elevate HR to this strategic level risks creating a socio-technical gap where advanced technologies are implemented into an unprepared organization, inevitably leading to friction, underperformance, and human cost.

To fulfill this strategic mandate, HR professionals themselves require a new suite of competencies. The literature points to a need for enhanced data literacy to understand and leverage people analytics, a sophisticated grasp of AI ethics to guide responsible implementation, and deep expertise in change management and organizational design. Operationally, this translates into redesigning core HR functions. Recruitment processes may be augmented by AI, but HR must ensure these tools are fair and non-discriminatory. Learning and development must pivot from periodic training to fostering a culture of continuous learning, providing platforms for on-demand upskilling and reskilling. Performance management systems must also evolve to evaluate human-AI collaboration and reward adaptability and learning, rather than focusing solely on traditional productivity metrics. In essence, HR must architect the organizational ecosystem that enables both humans and AI to thrive together.

Furthermore, our review highlights that organizational culture is not a passive backdrop but an active moderator that can either be a protective factor or a significant risk factor in the context of AI transformation. A culture characterized by psychological safety, where employees feel safe to experiment, ask questions, and even fail without fear of reprisal, creates a fertile ground for adapting to new technologies. Such a culture acts as a powerful organizational resource, buffering the demands of change. Conversely, a rigid, hierarchical, and blame-oriented culture will amplify technostress, as employees will perceive new technologies as a threat to their stability and competence. The concept of "digital culture maturity" thus becomes a critical indicator of an organization's readiness for AI and its potential for developing resilience. HR holds the primary responsibility for assessing and actively cultivating a culture that supports digital transformation.

Despite the breadth of this review, several limitations must be acknowledged. Firstly, the included studies were highly heterogeneous in terms of methodology, theoretical framing, and the specific technologies examined under the umbrella of "AI." This inherent diversity, while reflective of an emerging field, precludes meta-analytic conclusions and necessitates a narrative synthesis that prioritizes thematic patterns over quantitative aggregation. Secondly, a potential geographic and sectoral bias exists within the literature, with a large proportion of studies originating from North America and Europe and focusing on knowledge-based industries. The findings may therefore be less generalizable to other cultural contexts or to sectors such as manufacturing or agriculture, where the human-AI interface may differ substantially. Finally, the possibility of publication bias, wherein studies with statistically significant findings are more likely to be published, cannot be discounted. This may lead to an over-representation of both strongly positive and strongly negative outcomes in the literature.

These limitations illuminate several critical directions for future research. The prevalence of cross-sectional studies in the reviewed literature highlights a pressing need for more longitudinal research. Tracking organizations and employees over the entire lifecycle of an AI implementation—from pre-deployment through to mature usage—would provide invaluable insights into the dynamic and evolving nature of technostress, adaptation, and the effectiveness of HR interventions over time. Such studies could help disentangle causality and identify critical inflection points where interventions are most impactful. This methodological shift is crucial for moving beyond static snapshots to a more nuanced, process-oriented understanding of AI-driven transformation and the development of organizational resilience.

Finally, future research must focus more explicitly on designing and evaluating specific interventions. While this review identified the strategic importance of HR, there is a dearth of empirical studies that rigorously test the effectiveness of different HR strategies for mitigating technostress and building resilience in AI-rich environments. Future research could employ quasi-experimental designs to compare the outcomes of different training programs, change communication strategies, or job redesign initiatives. Furthermore, there is a significant need for more context-specific research that explores how the dynamics identified in this review play out in different types of organizations (e.g., small and medium-sized enterprises vs. large multinational corporations), different sectors (e.g., healthcare vs. finance), and diverse national cultures. Such research would provide practitioners with more targeted, evidence-based guidance for navigating their unique AI transformation journeys.

## 5. Conclusion

**Conclusion**

This scoping review set out to map the existing literature and answer the central research question: How does the scholarly evidence characterize the role of Human Resources (HR) in mitigating the negative impacts of AI, such as technostress, while leveraging its benefits to foster organizational resilience? The synthesis of the reviewed articles reveals a complex and dualistic landscape where AI acts as both an enabling and an alienating force within the workplace. The findings underscore that the transition to an AI-driven environment is not merely a technological implementation but a profound socio-technical challenge that places HR at the nexus of strategy, technology, and employee well-being. The evidence demonstrates that a passive or purely administrative approach from HR is insufficient; instead, a proactive, strategic, and human-centric leadership role is essential for navigating this transformation successfully and building a resilient organization capable of thriving amidst digital disruption.

The key findings of this review can be summarized into several critical points. First, the literature consistently affirms the "Janus face" of AI in HRM, recognizing its significant potential to streamline decision-making and enhance performance while simultaneously posing substantial risks of technostress, psychological distress, and employee alienation. Second, despite the recognized potential, the practical implementation of AI in HRM functions is currently limited and faces considerable barriers, including a lack of granular insight into implementation experiences and a need for greater expertise among HR professionals. Third, successful AI integration is contingent upon strategic, human-centric frameworks that prioritize transparent communication, stakeholder involvement, ethical considerations, and phased implementation, with HR leading these initiatives. For HR practitioners, the implication is clear: their role must evolve from operational administrators to strategic architects of change. This involves designing and championing programs like resilience training and establishing continuous feedback mechanisms to prepare the workforce and mitigate the psychosocial risks associated with AI adoption.

These findings carry significant implications for both organizational policy and future research. Internally, organizations must develop robust governance policies that align with emerging regulatory standards, such as the EU AI Act's focus on trustworthy and human-centric AI. Furthermore, existing Occupational Safety and Health (OSH) regulations must be updated or reinterpreted to explicitly include and address the psychosocial risks stemming from AI-induced technostress. The identified research gaps—including the limited granular discussion on implementation learnings and the need for more holistic studies examining HR's agency—provide a clear agenda for future scholarly inquiry. Ultimately, this review confirms that organizational resilience in the age of AI is not an automatic outcome of technological investment. It is, rather, a deliberately cultivated capability nurtured by strategic HR leadership that places human well-being at the core of technological transformation, thereby turning the disruptive potential of AI into a sustainable competitive advantage.
