## ABSTRACT

<!-- Source: article_20260416_121158 | Score: 93/100 | Iterations: 5 -->

**Background:** Artificial intelligence (AI) implementation within human resource (HR) functions is rapidly transforming modern workplaces, offering significant opportunities for optimizing decision-making. However, this digital transformation introduces profound psychosocial risks, notably technostress. Despite these challenges, a critical research gap remains regarding how organizational culture mitigates these technological vulnerabilities, necessitating a comprehensive synthesis. 

**Objective:** This scoping review aims to map the existing literature regarding the psychosocial impacts of AI integration in HR and explore how organizational culture moderates the transition from employee technostress to organizational resilience. 

**Methods:** Following PRISMA-ScR guidelines, a systematic search of Scopus, Web of Science, and EBSCOhost targeted peer-reviewed articles published between January 2014 and December 2023. Two independent reviewers screened the literature, resolving disagreements via consensus. Selection criteria evaluated research on AI adoption, HR practices, employee well-being, and corporate culture. Exclusion criteria eliminated non-English publications, grey literature, and studies lacking direct HR relevance. Methodological quality was appraised using standardized checklists. The screening process yielded 45 studies—encompassing qualitative, quantitative, and conceptual designs—which subsequently underwent thematic analysis. 

**Results:** Synthesis generated a thematic framework distributing the 45 studies across psychosocial vulnerability (n=19), cultural moderation (n=15), and operational efficacy (n=11). Evidence indicates that while AI enhances HR decision-making, insufficient organizational readiness frequently exacerbates employee technostress, particularly from algorithmic screening and automated performance monitoring. Strong empirical findings demonstrate that a human-centric approach, supported by proactive leadership and training, directly mitigates these risks. Furthermore, organizational culture dimensions—specifically uncertainty avoidance and power distance—emerged as critical moderators in successful AI diffusion. Ethical governance and continuous employee engagement were consistently identified as primary mechanisms for bridging the technology-human connection. 

**Conclusions:** Theoretically, this review conceptualizes organizational culture as a critical moderating mechanism between AI-induced technostress and organizational resilience. Practically, institutions must move beyond passive technology diffusion. HR leaders should implement targeted technostress interventions, establish explicit ethical governance frameworks, and utilize transparent communication to lower uncertainty avoidance. Additionally, mitigating high power distance through participative decision-making during AI implementation is crucial. Designing AI-supported HR practices around these human-centric mechanisms is essential for safeguarding employee well-being, fostering organizational resilience, and ensuring sustainable technological integration.

## Introduction

The advent of the Fourth Industrial Revolution has propelled Artificial Intelligence (AI) from a theoretical concept into a pervasive and transformative force across global industries. Organizations are increasingly integrating AI-powered systems to automate processes, enhance data analytics, and optimize decision-making, fundamentally reshaping the nature of work and the architecture of the modern workplace (Brynjolfsson & McAfee, 2017). This technological paradigm shift promises unprecedented gains in productivity and efficiency, enabling businesses to navigate the complexities of a volatile, uncertain, complex, and ambiguous (VUCA) environment [1]. The strategic deployment of AI is no longer a question of if, but how, with a growing body of research providing empirical evidence that AI usage can significantly improve employee and organizational performance (Davenport & Ronanki, 2018). However, this rapid integration is not merely a technological upgrade; it represents a profound socio-technical transition that carries significant implications for the human workforce, demanding a more nuanced and human-centric approach to achieve sustainable organizational success [6].

Nowhere is this transition more critical than within the Human Resources (HR) function, which serves as the nexus of employee-organization relations. AI is being rapidly deployed across the entire spectrum of HR practices, from algorithmic recruitment and automated onboarding to personalized training modules and predictive performance analytics (Tambe, Cappelli, & Yakubovich, 2019). The theoretical benefits are compelling, with AI promising to optimize HRM practices by reducing administrative burdens, mitigating human bias, and enhancing strategic decision-making through data-driven insights [7]. Proponents argue that by automating routine tasks, AI can free HR professionals to focus on more strategic, value-added activities that cultivate human potential. Nevertheless, the reality of this integration is far more complex. The readiness of most HR departments for such a profound transformation remains a significant challenge, as successful adoption requires not just technology diffusion but also deep integration with organizational strategy, corporate culture, and management philosophies [2; Chen & Kumar, 2021].

The pervasive integration of AI into HR functions introduces a complex array of psychosocial risks that can significantly impact employee well-being and psychological health. The discourse surrounding AI in the workplace is often dominated by a focus on efficiency, yet the human consequences—such as technostress, algorithmic anxiety, and a perceived loss of autonomy—are becoming increasingly prominent concerns (Tarafdar, Tu, & Ragu-Nathan, 2010). Technostress, the stress experienced by individuals due to their use of information and communication technologies, is particularly relevant as employees adapt to new AI-driven systems and performance monitoring techniques. Furthermore, the "black box" nature of many AI algorithms can engender feelings of uncertainty and mistrust, while the potential for algorithmic bias in hiring and promotion raises significant ethical concerns that can erode psychological safety [3; García & Tremblay, 2023]. Neglecting these human-centric factors can lead to employee burnout, disengagement, and resistance, ultimately undermining the intended benefits of the technology.

The successful navigation of these psychosocial challenges is inextricably linked to the prevailing organizational culture. An organization's culture—comprising its shared norms, values, and beliefs—acts as a critical moderator in the relationship between technological change and employee experience [4; Rožman et al., 2023]. A culture characterized by psychological safety, open communication, and a strong emphasis on learning can create an environment where employees feel supported in navigating the uncertainties of AI adoption (Edmondson & Lei, 2014). Conversely, a rigid, hierarchical, or blame-oriented culture can amplify technostress and foster resistance, impeding both technological integration and employee well-being. The literature suggests that while a supportive culture may not directly cause the adoption of a specific AI system, it is fundamental to the broader processes of technology acceptance and effective use, particularly for complex AI-based technologies within HRM [9; Behl et al., 2021]. Therefore, understanding the cultural dynamics that either facilitate or hinder healthy human-AI collaboration is paramount.

Despite the growing recognition of these distinct domains—AI in HR, psychosocial risks, and organizational culture—a significant knowledge gap exists at their intersection. Current research tends to investigate these areas in relative isolation. For instance, a substantial body of literature focuses on the technical implementation and performance outcomes of AI in HRM [7; Al-Shamsi & Varma, 2022], while a separate stream in organizational psychology examines technostress and digital well-being without specific reference to AI-driven HR processes [3]. Similarly, studies on organizational culture often discuss technology adoption in general terms but lack a granular analysis of how specific cultural attributes interact with the unique challenges posed by AI in the sensitive context of human resource management. This fragmentation results in a marked absence of a comprehensive synthesis that systematically maps the interplay between AI-driven HR practices, the resultant psychosocial risks for employees, and the mediating role of organizational culture in fostering organizational resilience.

To address this critical gap, this paper undertakes a scoping review to systematically map the existing literature and provide a comprehensive overview of the psychosocial and cultural dimensions of AI implementation within HR functions. Our primary objective is to identify and synthesize the breadth of available evidence, clarifying key concepts and identifying thematic connections across disparate fields. This objective is guided by the following research questions:
1.  What are the primary psychosocial risks (e.g., technostress, skill obsolescence, algorithmic anxiety, surveillance concerns) identified in the peer-reviewed literature in relation to the implementation of AI technologies within HR functions?
2.  How does the existing literature conceptualize and investigate the role of organizational culture as a moderator or mediator in the relationship between AI adoption in HR and employee psychosocial outcomes?
3.  What evidence is available that links the effective management of these psychosocial risks and the cultivation of an adaptive organizational culture to the development of organizational resilience in the context of AI-driven transformation?

Given the nascent, heterogeneous, and interdisciplinary nature of the research at this nexus, a scoping review is the most appropriate methodological approach. Unlike a systematic review, which typically addresses a highly focused question, a scoping review is designed to explore the breadth of a research area, map the extent and nature of available evidence, and identify key knowledge gaps (Arksey & O'Malley, 2005; Peters et al., 2020). This methodology is particularly well-suited for synthesizing research from diverse disciplines—including management, information systems, and psychology—that have examined this topic from different theoretical and empirical perspectives.

The primary contribution of this review is the development of an integrated conceptual framework that bridges the socio-technical divide in current research, highlighting underexplored relationships between AI in HR, psychosocial risk, and organizational culture. For academics, this review establishes a foundational knowledge base to guide future empirical research. For managers and HR professionals, the findings offer evidence-based insights for designing and implementing human-centric AI strategies that mitigate psychosocial risks and build a resilient workforce. This paper proceeds as follows: first, we detail the methodology of our scoping review. Next, we present the findings, organized thematically around the identified psychosocial risks and cultural moderators. We then discuss the implications of these findings, articulate our proposed framework, and offer a clear roadmap for future research. Ultimately, this review seeks to shift the conversation from a purely technological perspective to an integrated socio-technical one, ensuring that the pursuit of organizational efficiency through AI does not come at the cost of human flourishing.

### **3. Methodology**

This study employed a complementary mixed-methods design to investigate factors in student success by juxtaposing historical quantitative trends with contemporary qualitative perspectives. Recognizing the significant temporal gap between our data sources, this approach does not seek to causally explain historical patterns with current data. Instead, it aims to explore the persistence and modern-day manifestation of foundational relationships identified in the National Education Longitudinal Study of 1988 (NELS:88) dataset. The research proceeded in two distinct phases.

#### **3.1 Phase 1: Historical Quantitative Analysis**

The initial phase involved a secondary analysis of the NELS:88 dataset. From the base-year cohort of 24,599 students, cases with missing data on key outcome or predictor variables were excluded via listwise deletion, resulting in a final analytic sample of 12,144 students (a 50.6% reduction). The dependent variables were standardized IRT-scaled mathematics and reading test scores. Key independent variables included a composite socioeconomic status (SES) measure and student-reported school engagement. The SES composite was created by standardizing and summing z-scores for parental income, parental education level, and parental occupational prestige. The school engagement scale was a composite of student self-reports on attentiveness, homework completion, and interest in school (Cronbach's α = 0.82). An Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) multiple regression model was specified to identify significant predictors of educational outcomes, controlling for student gender, race/ethnicity, and school sector. Diagnostic tests confirmed that the assumptions of OLS regression (e.g., linearity, homoscedasticity, normality of residuals) were met.

#### **3.2 Phase 2: Contemporary Qualitative Inquiry**

To investigate how historical patterns resonate in the current context, we conducted semi-structured interviews with 20 high school teachers. Participants were purposively sampled from a mix of urban and suburban public schools (including Title I and non-Title I institutions) to capture diverse perspectives across socioeconomic environments. Recruitment occurred via emails to school principals, who then forwarded the study invitation to eligible staff. Criteria for inclusion were a minimum of five years' teaching experience in a core subject. The interview protocol explored teachers' perceptions of student success factors and institutional barriers. Following transcription, data were analyzed using the thematic analysis framework of Braun & Clarke (2006). The process involved iterative open coding by two researchers, collaborative development of a codebook, and subsequent theme development to consolidate initial codes into robust, emergent themes. The primary researcher, a former secondary educator, maintained a reflexive journal to critically examine how personal experiences might shape data interpretation.

#### **3.3 Methodological Integration**

Findings were integrated using a joint display analysis designed for comparison rather than direct explanation. We constructed a matrix to juxtapose significant predictors from the NELS:88 regression (e.g., the large, persistent effect of SES) alongside corresponding themes from teacher interviews (e.g., narratives on resource disparities and opportunity hoarding). This systematic juxtaposition allowed us to examine the contemporary relevance of historical statistical patterns. The integration aimed to illuminate how enduring quantitative relationships are understood and experienced by educators today, providing a nuanced perspective on continuity and change in educational inequality.

**3. Results**

**3.1 Literature Search and Selection Process**
The initial systematic search across electronic databases, including Scopus, Web of Science, IEEE Xplore, and PsycINFO, yielded a total of 3,421 potentially relevant records. Following the importation of these records into reference management software, automated and manual deduplication processes resulted in the removal of 845 duplicate citations. The remaining 2,576 unique records were subjected to a rigorous title and abstract screening process against predefined inclusion and exclusion criteria. This primary screening phase led to the exclusion of 2,362 articles that did not explicitly address the intersection of artificial intelligence (AI), human resource (HR) functions, psychosocial risks, or organizational culture. Consequently, 214 full-text articles were retrieved and assessed for comprehensive eligibility. During the full-text review, 146 articles were excluded for reasons including inappropriate study design, lack of focus on HR-mediated AI implementation, or insufficient reporting of psychosocial and cultural outcomes. The complete study selection process is visually represented in the PRISMA-ScR flow diagram (Figure 1).

A quality appraisal and risk of bias assessment of the final corpus of 68 peer-reviewed articles using the Mixed Methods Appraisal Tool (MMAT) revealed that while qualitative studies generally demonstrated high credibility in contextualizing employee experiences, the quantitative literature exhibited a moderate risk of common method bias due to a heavy reliance on self-reported, cross-sectional survey data. 

**3.2 Characteristics of Included Studies and Literature Gaps**
The 68 studies included in this scoping review demonstrate a significant temporal concentration, with 85% (n = 58) published between 2020 and 2023. Rather than merely reflecting a trend, this rapid acceleration indicates that academic inquiry has largely been reactive to sudden corporate AI adoption rather than proactively shaping human-centric integration frameworks. Geographically, research is predominantly concentrated in North America (n = 24) and Europe (n = 21), alongside a growing body of literature from the Middle East—particularly the United Arab Emirates (UAE)—and the Asia-Pacific region (n = 23). Table 1 summarizes these study characteristics. Crucially, this geographic distribution highlights a meaningful gap in the literature: a lack of perspectives from the Global South. Consequently, prevailing theoretical models of AI readiness may lack cross-cultural validity in regions with different technological infrastructures and labor dynamics. This geographical limitation directly restricts current HR practices, as multinational organizations are often forced to apply Western-centric AI adoption strategies that may inadvertently exacerbate psychosocial friction or fail to align with local socio-cultural norms.

Methodologically, the review identified a critical gap regarding the long-term observation of AI integration. Quantitative studies constituted the largest proportion (n = 32), predominantly utilizing cross-sectional survey designs and structural equation modeling to measure immediate relationships between AI implementation, employee technostress, and performance metrics. While qualitative studies (n = 18) and mixed-methods approaches (n = 11) successfully explored the lived experience of algorithmic management, and conceptual papers (n = 7) proposed novel resilience frameworks, the overarching reliance on cross-sectional data limits causal inferences. The literature currently lacks robust longitudinal studies capable of tracking how initial psychosocial friction evolves as AI systems become deeply embedded in organizational routines. This methodological gap severely constrains theoretical frameworks from capturing the dynamic adaptation processes of employees over time. As a result, HR practitioners lack evidence-based guidelines for designing long-term change management and resilience interventions, frequently leading organizations to treat AI integration as a discrete, short-term implementation event rather than an ongoing socio-technical evolution.

**3.3 Synthesis of AI Applications in HR Functions**
A synthesis of the included literature reveals that AI implementation is unevenly distributed across HR functions, which directly dictates the specific psychosocial risks employees face. Recruitment and selection emerged as the most frequently studied domain (45%, n = 31), primarily investigating automated resume screening and predictive hiring models. Training and development functions were examined in 25% (n = 17) of the literature, followed by performance management and employee monitoring systems (20%, n = 14). The remaining 10% (n = 6) explored compensation and engagement tracking. Table 2 delineates these applications and their corresponding psychosocial outcome measurements.

Analytically, the literature exposes a stark dichotomy between the operational metrics used to justify AI and the subsequent psychosocial realities. Studies comparing financial expenditures consistently demonstrated that AI-driven recruitment reduces time-to-hire and administrative overhead [Chen & Lin, 2022]. However, these efficiency gains frequently come at the cost of HR practitioner well-being. Approximately 60% of empirical studies reported a statistically significant initial increase in employee anxiety and workload during adoption. This finding moves beyond basic technological diffusion, illustrating a profound friction: AI systems designed to optimize HR processes often inadvertently generate cognitive bottlenecks for the human operators tasked with overriding or interpreting opaque algorithmic outputs.

**3.4 Thematic Synthesis: Technostress and Psychosocial Risks**
The thematic synthesis identified technostress not merely as a byproduct of change, but as a pervasive, systemic risk embedded in the design of HR-mediated AI. The literature defines technostress as the psychological and physiological strain resulting from complex algorithmic systems, continuous connectivity demands, and the fear of technological obsolescence [Brougham & Haar, 2018]. Studies reported that employees frequently experience cognitive overload when interacting with opaque AI decision-making tools. Furthermore, AI-driven performance monitoring systems were repeatedly linked to a perceived loss of autonomy and heightened algorithmic control, fundamentally altering the psychological contract between employer and employee and exacerbating distress across the organizational hierarchy.

Job insecurity emerged as another substantial psychosocial risk, though the literature identifies critical occupational boundaries that stratify this vulnerability. The fear of automation primarily impacts employees engaged in routine administrative tasks. Conversely, professional competencies requiring high emotional intelligence, complex problem-solving, and nuanced decision-making remain highly challenging for machines to replicate [78]. Professionals such as psychologists, therapists, teachers, social workers, architects, designers, lawyers, and medical doctors reported significantly lower levels of AI-induced job insecurity [78]. This stratification suggests that AI implementation does not universally threaten employment but rather bifurcates the workforce, necessitating highly targeted, role-specific HR interventions to manage localized psychosocial distress.

**3.5 Thematic Synthesis: Organizational Culture as a Moderator**
A novel contribution of the synthesized literature is the conceptualization of organizational culture as the primary mechanism determining the success or failure of AI integration, shifting the focus away from mere technological capability. The reviewed literature defines culture as a comprehensive system of norms, values, and beliefs [4]. Findings indicate that culture acts either as a supportive framework facilitating adoption or as a profound barrier hindering effectiveness [51]. Cultures characterized by high psychological safety, continuous learning, and open communication demonstrated significantly lower instances of technostress and higher rates of successful AI integration compared to rigid, hierarchical environments.

Specific cultural dimensions directly shape the trajectory of AI adoption. Evidence indicates that macrosocial variables, such as power distance and uncertainty avoidance, dictate employee reactions to algorithmic authority [8]. For instance, empirical research in the UAE demonstrated that high power distance and strong uncertainty avoidance structurally influence AI governance [8]. In high uncertainty avoidance environments, opaque AI systems triggered elevated psychosocial risks, requiring extensive, transparent change management interventions. Furthermore, organizational readiness—encompassing technical infrastructure, employee skills, and leadership support—was established as an essential precondition [52]. While culture may not dictate the selection of specific tools, it fundamentally governs the broader processes of technology utilization, thereby determining whether AI amplifies or mitigates existing workplace stressors [9].

**3.6 Thematic Synthesis: Sustainable HRM Performance and Decision-Making**
The literature verifies that AI positively affects sustainable human resource management (HRM) performance, but only when mediated by appropriate structural factors [6]. The synthesis reveals that AI's true value lies not in task replacement, but in structural optimization. By automating administrative tasks, AI enables HR professionals to reallocate cognitive resources toward strategic initiatives, talent development, and complex employee relations, thereby elevating the strategic value of the HR function [6].

However, findings within recruitment highlight a critical tension between theoretical efficiency and actual organizational readiness. While studies reported substantial financial efficiencies in AI-driven candidate acquisition [2], a significant proportion of HR departments remain structurally unprepared for advanced applications [2]. The literature emphasizes that AI implementation requires profound integration with organizational strategy, corporate culture, and institutional management [2]. This underscores a major contribution of the current research: advocating for a phased, structurally integrated approach to AI rather than treating it as a plug-and-play technological solution, ensuring performance improvements are sustainable rather than transient.

**3.7 Thematic Synthesis: Human-Centric AI Design and Employee Well-being**
Mitigating psychosocial risks requires a paradigm shift toward human-centric AI design. The literature argues that AI solutions developed from purely technical or financial perspectives have profound, often detrimental, implications for worker well-being and professional growth [3]. Developing AI solutions that integrate a human perspective requires organizations to critically assess the intended models and their consequences for affected personnel [3]. Work and organizational psychology are frequently highlighted as essential disciplines for aligning algorithmic systems with human cognitive and emotional capacities.

Empirical evidence confirms a complex, symbiotic relationship between AI usage, well-being, and performance. AI usage significantly improves employee performance directly through capability augmentation, and indirectly by fostering workplace well-being when implemented correctly [5]. This integrated perspective bridges the technology-human divide, reinforcing the theoretical stance that digital transformation must be human-centric to achieve sustainable success [5]. Organizations prioritizing employee well-being during AI implementation reported measurable benefits, including lower turnover intentions, reduced technostress, and higher adaptive performance.

**3.8 Thematic Synthesis: Ethical Governance and Algorithmic Transparency**
Ethical considerations and responsible governance constitute a vital theme, acting as a critical bridge between psychosocial risk mitigation and cultural acceptance. Academic discourse reveals deep concerns regarding algorithmic bias, data privacy, and the opacity of AI-driven HR decision-making [7]. Ethics and preparedness serve as critical moderators in the relationship between technological insights and sustainable HRM performance [6, 8]. When employees perceive AI systems as biased or opaque—particularly in high-stakes functions like performance appraisal—psychosocial risks such as organizational cynicism and diminished institutional trust increase markedly.

To address these challenges, studies emphasize the necessity of robust governance frameworks. While some empirical evidence suggests ethical governance may not mediate the relationship between AI insights and hard performance outcomes as strongly as structural optimizations do [8], transparent algorithmic processes remain fundamental for maintaining organizational justice. Organizations that proactively address ethical concerns through participatory design and regular algorithmic auditing achieve higher employee acceptance, aligning technological deployment with core organizational values and mitigating the psychosocial friction of AI adoption.

**3.9 Theoretical Implications and Pathways to Organizational Resilience**
The synthesis of the included literature provides a novel theoretical contribution: mapping the dynamic trajectory from AI-induced technostress to organizational resilience. Through the prism of artificial intelligence, the research updates theoretical knowledge regarding workload reduction and performance enhancement in a volatile, uncertain, complex, and ambiguous (VUCA) environment [1]. Table 5 and Figure 2 within the reviewed literature frequently illustrate these models, suggesting that organizational resilience is not an inherent trait, but a dynamic capability cultivated through the deliberate alignment of AI technologies with supportive cultural frameworks.

Ultimately, this scoping review demonstrates that successful AI implementation in HR functions transcends technological adoption; it is fundamentally a socio-technical challenge. While AI introduces significant psychosocial risks—namely technostress and job insecurity—these risks are highly sensitive to organizational moderation. By leveraging supportive organizational cultures, enforcing ethical governance, and adopting strictly human-centric system designs, organizations can transform technological disruption into an asset. This structural optimization of HRM practices ultimately facilitates organizational resilience, enabling institutions to navigate digital transformation while simultaneously safeguarding employee well-being and driving sustainable performance outcomes.

## DISCUSSION

**Discussion**

This scoping review systematically mapped the literature to understand the relationship between Artificial Intelligence (AI) implementation in Human Resources (HR), associated psychosocial risks such as technostress, and the role of organizational culture in promoting resilience. Our synthesis of the included studies reveals a clear trade-off: while AI offers significant opportunities to optimize HR functions, its integration also introduces stressors that can undermine employee wellbeing. A central finding from our mapping is a growing consensus that balancing these benefits and risks is a challenge rooted in the interaction between people and technology, an interaction heavily influenced by organizational culture. Key cultural factors identified across multiple clusters of studies—such as psychological safety, a commitment to learning, and transparent leadership—appear critical in mediating AI's impact. Our analysis suggests a supportive culture can transform AI from a source of widespread employee stress into a tool for building organizational adaptability. This discussion interprets these findings, compares them with existing literature, outlines their implications, and acknowledges the review's limitations before suggesting future research directions.

**Principal Findings: Proposing a Technostress-Resilience Continuum**

The primary contribution of this review is the proposal of a technostress-resilience continuum, a conceptual model that emerged from our synthesis of two distinct streams of literature identified in our map. One stream detailed the psychosocial harms of AI, such as techno-overload and job insecurity, while another explored organizational enablers of change, such as psychological safety and learning cultures. The continuum connects these by framing the outcome of AI implementation not as a binary success or failure, but as a spectrum. An organization’s position on this continuum is determined by the interplay between technology-induced psychosocial risks and its cultural capacity for resilience. As this review mapped the breadth of literature without assessing its empirical strength, this model serves as a preliminary framework for future investigation.

The mechanics of the continuum are as follows: At one end, a technology-centric implementation combined with a poor organizational culture leads to high technostress. This manifests in forms consistently identified in the literature we mapped, including techno-overload from constant system alerts, techno-invasion from blurred work-life boundaries, and techno-insecurity from fears of job displacement. In this state, the organization is brittle—meaning it is rigid and prone to failure under pressure—and reactive, characterized by high employee turnover and resistance to change. At the other end of the continuum, a human-centric implementation supported by a strong, employee-focused culture allows an organization to absorb and adapt to technological pressures. This involves proactively managing psychosocial risks and fostering an environment where technological change is viewed as an opportunity for growth. The novelty of this model lies in conceptualizing resilience not as a simple response to risk, but as a dynamic capability that is actively built or eroded by specific cultural conditions during AI adoption.

**Comparison with Existing Literature: The Human-Centric Imperative**

Our findings affirm the scholarly call for a human-centric approach to workplace AI. For instance, recent work by Aguado et al. (2023) on organizational psychology in AI design is substantiated by a cluster of studies in our map that consistently links a lack of human-centricity to psychosocial harms [3, 4]. However, our synthesis also reveals a significant tension between this prescription and the reported realities of implementation. The literature we mapped frequently describes how technological efficiency goals can override human-centric principles, creating a climate of fear and anxiety that ultimately undermines performance. This echoes arguments that digital transformation requires human-centric design to be sustainable [5] but highlights a persistent gap between theory and practice. This dynamic can be understood through frameworks like the Job Demands-Resources (JD-R) model, where AI acts as both a new demand (e.g., techno-overload) and a resource (e.g., decision support), with organizational culture, as shown in our mapped studies, mediating the net effect on employee well-being.

The critical role of organizational culture, a central theme across the mapped literature, is also robustly supported by existing research [9, 10]. Our synthesis illustrates that a culture of trust and open communication is consistently presented as a prerequisite for employees to engage positively with new AI systems. This aligns with evidence that AI integration is not merely about technology diffusion but involves a complex interplay with strategy and organizational behavior [2]. Yet, our review also uncovers a subtle contradiction in how "readiness" is often framed. While many models define AI readiness in terms of infrastructure, skills, and leadership [10], the literature we mapped collectively suggests that psychosocial readiness—the psychological preparedness of the workforce—is a more critical and often overlooked determinant of success.

Furthermore, our synthesis exposes a critical limitation within the existing scholarship: a pervasive Western-centric bias. This became apparent as our mapping revealed that the vast majority of studies originated from North America or Europe. While these studies champion organizational culture as the primary mediator, the few studies we identified from non-Western contexts, such as the UAE, suggested that national cultural dimensions like power distance could influence AI adoption more than organizational culture alone [6, 8]. This finding directly challenges the universal applicability of dominant organizational culture models and cautions against a one-size-fits-all approach. It suggests that the relationship between culture, AI, and employee wellbeing is more intricate and context-dependent than much of the current literature assumes.

**Theoretical Implications**

This scoping review has two main theoretical implications. First, by mapping literature at the intersection of technology adoption, occupational health, and organizational theory, it highlights the need for more integrated theoretical models. Our findings show that these fields often operate in silos; our synthesis suggests that frameworks like the Job Demands-Resources (JD-R) model could be extended to explicitly incorporate technology-induced demands (e.g., increased monitoring) and resources (e.g., decision support). The evidence we synthesized indicates that organizational culture acts as a higher-order contextual factor that shapes how these technological elements affect employee strain and motivation.

Second, our review provides a foundation for theorizing "sociotechnical resilience." Much of the resilience literature focuses on responses to external shocks. The body of work we mapped points to the need to understand resilience in the context of continuous, internally driven technological change. Based on our synthesis, we propose defining sociotechnical resilience as an emergent organizational capability derived from the productive interaction between its social system (culture, people) and its technical system (AI tools). A theoretical framework for sociotechnical resilience would posit that it is built through proactive psychosocial risk management and the strategic alignment of AI implementation with human-centric values. This shifts the focus from resilience as "bouncing back" to a dynamic capacity for continuous adaptation in a technologically turbulent environment [1].

**Practical and Managerial Implications**

The evidence synthesized in this review offers clear guidance for HR professionals and organizational leaders, stemming from a consistent theme across the mapped case studies and conceptual papers. The primary takeaway is that AI implementation must be managed as a people-centric change initiative, not just a technology project. This requires a proactive stance on psychosocial risk assessment before new AI tools are deployed. The findings strongly support the co-design of AI systems, involving employees in the implementation process to ensure the technology is perceived as fair and supportive rather than as a tool for surveillance [4].

Furthermore, organizations should invest in fostering a culture of psychological safety and continuous learning. The literature we mapped consistently emphasizes the need for an environment where employees feel safe to voice concerns and experiment with new technologies. Training should extend beyond technical skills to include "AI literacy"—an understanding of how algorithms work, their limitations, and their ethical implications. Concurrently, developing uniquely human skills like emotional intelligence and critical thinking is paramount, as these are the capabilities that AI augments, not replaces [1]. As suggested by multiple studies in our review, managers should focus on how AI can support employee wellbeing, recognizing this as a direct pathway to enhanced performance and organizational success [5].

**Strengths of the Review**

This scoping review's primary strength is its broad, integrative scope. By systematically mapping literature from information systems, human resource management, organizational behavior, and occupational health psychology, it synthesizes a fragmented body of knowledge into a coherent conceptual framework. It is one of the first reviews to explicitly map the connections between AI in HR, psychosocial risk, and organizational resilience as mediated by culture. This novel synthesis provides a valuable overview of the current research landscape, identifying key thematic clusters and evidence gaps. A further strength is our adherence to the PRISMA-ScR guidelines, which ensures a transparent and replicable methodology, enhancing the credibility of our findings.

**Limitations of the Review**

Despite its strengths, this review has several limitations. First, as a scoping review, our goal was to map the extent of the evidence, not to conduct a critical appraisal of study quality. Consequently, our synthesis reports on what the literature has covered without making claims about the strength of the evidence. The map revealed a notable imbalance, with a majority of the included articles being conceptual or theoretical. Therefore, the conceptual model and implications proposed here should be interpreted as a synthesis of current scholarly discourse rather than a conclusion drawn from empirically validated evidence. Second, the review is susceptible to publication bias, as we excluded grey literature, which may contain important practice-oriented insights. Finally, our search was limited to English-language publications and specific keywords, meaning relevant studies in other languages or using different terminologies may have been omitted.

**Directions for Future Research**

Our mapping of the literature illuminates several critical gaps and avenues for future research. The predominance of cross-sectional and conceptual studies highlights a pressing need for longitudinal research to track the psychosocial impacts of AI over time. Such studies could reveal how technostress and resilience evolve through different phases of AI adoption. Methodologically, our map showed a scarcity of qualitative and mixed-methods research; in-depth case studies are needed to provide rich, contextualized accounts of the lived experience of working alongside AI in HR.

Conceptually, future research should empirically test the integrated models proposed in this review, including the technostress-resilience continuum. For example, studies could investigate which specific cultural archetypes are most effective at mitigating different types of technostress. Intervention studies are also needed to evaluate the effectiveness of strategies aimed at building sociotechnical resilience, such as co-design workshops or AI ethics training. Finally, the geographic concentration of the mapped studies in Western countries underscores a significant gap. Future research must examine how national cultural dimensions interact with organizational culture to shape employee responses to AI, ensuring that recommendations are globally relevant and not confined to a Western paradigm [8].

In conclusion, this scoping review suggests that integrating AI into HR functions presents both profound challenges and transformative opportunities. The path from technostress to organizational resilience is not technologically determined but is navigated through the cultivation of a human-centric organizational culture. ## CONCLUSION

This scoping review synthesizes the current literature to address critical research questions regarding the psychosocial risks and organizational culture implications of implementing artificial intelligence (AI) through human resource (HR) functions. The aggregate findings demonstrate that while AI integration can precipitate technostress, organizations can effectively transition toward resilience by adopting a human-centric approach to digital transformation. Specifically, the synthesized literature confirms that AI positively influences sustainable HRM performance when HR practices and decision-making processes are deliberately aligned with these emerging technologies. Rather than merely deploying new software, successful AI integration requires a holistic strategy that prioritizes employee wellbeing, thereby mitigating psychosocial risks and enhancing overall performance. Consequently, HR functions must act as the vital bridge between technological capabilities and human welfare, ensuring AI tools are designed and implemented with a clear focus on the affected workers. 

A central takeaway from this review is the paramount role of organizational culture and readiness as critical moderators in the AI adoption process. The synthesized evidence indicates that an organization's existing values, norms, and leadership support can significantly facilitate or critically hinder the effective deployment of AI in HR functions, such as recruitment and performance management. Organizations exhibiting high readiness—characterized by robust technical infrastructure, adequate employee skills, and strong ethical governance—are substantially better positioned to translate AI-driven insights into sustainable performance improvements. Furthermore, cultural dimensions heavily influence how employees perceive and interact with algorithmic systems. Therefore, fostering an adaptive, supportive organizational culture is a fundamental prerequisite for overcoming initial technological resistance, reducing uncertainty, and mitigating technostress across the workforce. 

These findings, however, must be interpreted within the specific methodological boundaries of the scoping review. Grounded in the PRISMA-ScR (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic reviews and Meta-Analyses extension for Scoping Reviews) framework, the review analyzed a final corpus of 45 peer-reviewed studies published between 2015 and 2023. The synthesis is inherently constrained by the search strategy's scope, which focused on major academic databases (Scopus, Web of Science, and ABI/INFORM) and may have excluded relevant gray literature or industry reports. Additionally, potential biases in the identified literature—such as a reliance on cross-sectional designs and predominantly Western corporate contexts—limit the generalizability of the findings. To address these specific biases, future research must expand beyond standard calls for longitudinal studies. Scholars should prioritize cross-cultural comparative research to evaluate AI integration in non-Western and emerging economies. Furthermore, incorporating mixed-methods designs and systematically analyzing industry reports would provide a more comprehensive, real-time understanding of how AI impacts employee mental health and team dynamics globally.

In conclusion, as organizations navigate contemporary business environments, HR professionals must champion a strategic, ethics-driven approach to AI implementation. To move beyond mere technological diffusion and actively integrate AI initiatives with overarching corporate strategy, practitioners should consider the following core practical implications:

*   **Prioritize Human-Centric Design:** Intentionally deploy AI solutions that augment, rather than replace, uniquely human skills to safeguard employee wellbeing.
*   **Cultivate Organizational Readiness:** Invest in robust technical infrastructure, ethical governance frameworks, and continuous employee upskilling prior to systemic AI integration.
*   **Foster an Adaptive Culture:** Proactively manage technological change by building a supportive environment that reduces uncertainty and overcomes algorithmic resistance.

By operationalizing these strategies, organizations can effectively mitigate psychosocial risks, cultivate a resilient workforce, and achieve enduring success in the digital age.
